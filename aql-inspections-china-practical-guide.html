AQL Inspections in China — The Clear, Practical Version

Apr 21, 2026 · 8 min read

Plain-English AQL guide for China inspections: sampling levels, defect classes, DUPRO vs. pre-shipment, pass/fail examples, and what to do when results miss.

What AQL actually is (and why it exists)

AQL (Acceptable Quality Limit) is a sampling plan and a decision rule. You don't check every unit; you check a statistically chosen sample. If defects in that sample stay at or below the allowed count, the lot passes. If they exceed it, the lot fails and needs rework, replacement, or re-inspection. AQL prevents endless debates by defining "how many defects are too many" in advance.

The three defect classes you must name

Critical (CR): Safety, legality, or function failures (e.g., wrong voltage, sharp edge, missing safety label). Target is zero. Major (MA): Likely return or unusable (e.g., deep scratches on visible surface, non-working feature). Minor (MI): Cosmetic or small deviation that most buyers accept (e.g., tiny paint speck in non-critical area). Your PO should say which issues fall in each bucket. If you don't define them, the factory will — on the fly.

Picking a sampling level (without the math headache)

Use ANSI/ASQ Z1.4. You pick a general inspection level (II is standard for most orders), find your code letter from the lot size, then read the sample size and the accept/reject numbers for each defect class. A practical default for first runs: General level II, AQL thresholds CR = 0.0, MA = 2.5, MI = 4.0. That mix catches real problems without stalling every ship date. Lower AQL numbers = stricter (fewer defects allowed). Raise them only after consistent performance.

DUPRO vs. Pre-Shipment: do you need both?

DUPRO (during production): Early check while lines are running. Best place to catch assembly mistakes, finish drift, and packaging confusion — when fixes are still cheap. Pre-Shipment (PSI): Final check on packed or nearly packed goods. Confirms labeling, counts, barcodes, and carton integrity — right before release. For new suppliers or new SKUs, run both. Once the process is stable, you can lighten DUPRO frequency and keep PSI strict.

What a clean inspection plan looks like (copy/paste)

Order: 5,000 units · Level: General II (ANSI/ASQ Z1.4) · AQL: CR 0.0 / MA 2.5 / MI 4.0. Checklist: Function ✓, key dimensions ±0.3 mm, finish per sample, label/COO, barcode scans, pack per dieline, drop test 3 edges/1 corner. Golden sample: #GS-042, dated, signed by both parties. Photos: 20+ across sampling, defects, labeling, carton, pallet. Outcome: Pass/Fail with counts by defect class; re-inspect after rework if failed. Put this block in your PO.

Pass/Fail examples (to make it real)

Example A — Pass: Sample size = 200. Allowed at MA 2.5 is 10 defects; MI 4.0 is 14. Inspector finds 6 MA, 9 MI, 0 CR. Result: Pass. Example B — Fail: Same sample. Inspector finds 0 CR, 12 MA, 7 MI. Result: Fail (MA exceeds 10). Action: rework/replace items causing MA, re-inspect affected cartons. Example C — Automatic Fail: Any critical defect (e.g., wrong plug, missing warning label required by law). Result: Fail regardless of MA/MI counts.

What to do when AQL results miss

Name the fix and the owner. Repaint, re-assemble, re-label — who does it, by when. Protect the schedule. Re-inspect only the affected scope if practical; don't restart the world. Raise the sample if borderline. A larger sample can statistically clear uncertainty without delaying a week. Prevent repeats. Feed defect photos and counts back into DFM or work instructions.

The quiet killer: vague "golden samples"

A golden sample that lives in someone's desk is not protection. Treat it like a mini-spec: date it, sign it, photograph it, and refer to it in the checklist. If the supplier wants to change a component, finish, or tolerance — approve a new golden before the line restarts.
